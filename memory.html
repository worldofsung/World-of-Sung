A message of love

To Masami and Shizuko — your love story began in one of the darkest chapters of American history, yet from that darkness bloomed something beautiful. Two souls brought together at the Japanese American internment camp, finding each other amid hardship and uncertainty. That chance meeting became the foundation of everything — a family, a legacy, a love that has carried across generations. Your courage, your resilience, and the love you built together lives on in every one of us. We carry you with us always.

歴史 · History

A timeline of courage

1918 · Tottori, Japan
Masano Yonemura receives emigration permit
Granted permission by Tottori Prefecture to travel to America. A young woman with a dream, documented in beautiful Japanese calligraphy.
January 17, 1919 · San Francisco
Arrival in America aboard the SS Shinyo Maru
Masano arrives from Yokohama, Japan. Visa issued by the American Consulate General in Yokohama. She settles in Long Beach, California.
April 30, 1921 · Long Beach, CA
Shizuko Lillie Nakamura is born
Born in Long Beach to Japanese immigrant parents. She grows up as a Nisei — second-generation Japanese American.
1942 · Santa Anita & Jerome, Arkansas
Japanese American Internment
Following Executive Order 9066, Shizuko — like over 120,000 Japanese Americans — was forcibly relocated. She worked as a stenographer and later a timekeeper at the Jerome Relocation Center. Her documents from this period survive as a testament to this dark chapter in American history.
Post-War · Long Beach, CA
The family returns and rebuilds
The Yonemura family roots themselves in Long Beach at 2709 Dale Ave — the address on a letter preserved in this archive. They rebuild their lives with grace and quiet strength.
Today · The Sung Family
Their legacy lives on
Through Lindsey Masami Yonemura-Sung, and her children Levi Ryota and Aiden Kaito — the Yonemura spirit, strength, and love continue across generations.
